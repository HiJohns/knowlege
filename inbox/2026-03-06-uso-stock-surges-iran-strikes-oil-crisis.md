# USO stock surges 25% and traders want answers

> **摘要**：伊朗无人机袭击卡塔尔能源设施导致原油飙升8%，USO ETF一周内上涨约25%，但分析师对涨势能否持续存在分歧，地缘政治因素使油价预测变得更加复杂。

## 正文内容

Iranian drone strikes on Qatar's energy hubs sent crude surging 8% and put the world's most-watched oil ETF back in the spotlight.

The United States Oil Fund, known on trading screens as USO, has become one of the most-watched tickers on Wall Street this year. This week erased any doubt about why. The exchange-traded fund surged nearly 7% in a single week and now sits roughly 25% above where it started in January, driven by a geopolitical shock that rattled energy markets in a way not seen in years.

Iranian drone strikes on QatarEnergy facilities at Ras Laffan and Mesaieed forced a full halt of liquefied natural gas production at two of the country's most critical export hubs. Saudi Aramco's Ras Tanura refinery shut down as a precaution. Brent crude jumped 8% in a single session. Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil supply passes, slowed to near standstill. For energy markets, it was the kind of morning that rewrites price forecasts before lunch.

### What USO actually does

USO does not own oil wells or tankers. The fund, managed by USCF Investments and listed on NYSE Arca, holds front-month crude oil futures contracts tied to West Texas Intermediate, the American benchmark for crude pricing. When oil prices move, USO moves with them, which makes the ETF a widely used tool for traders who want oil exposure without navigating derivatives markets directly.

That accessibility comes with a structural quirk. Because futures contracts expire monthly, USO must continuously roll its holdings forward into the next contract. Under normal market conditions, when future prices sit above near-term prices, that rolling process quietly erodes returns. Over the past five years, USO returned just 0.21% despite oil prices swinging dramatically in both directions.

The Iran-Qatar crisis changed that dynamic, at least temporarily. Acute supply shocks can flip the futures curve into what traders call backwardation, a condition where near-term contracts trade at a premium over longer-dated ones. In those environments, USO's rolling structure works in its favor rather than against it, and that shift partly explains the ETF's sharp move this week.

### A disruption that goes beyond the headlines

Qatar's forced production shutdown affected roughly 20% of global LNG supply, a disruption large enough to send force majeure declarations rippling through energy contracts worldwide. The Strait of Hormuz, already a chronic pressure point in global shipping, became an active flashpoint almost overnight.

The fund holds approximately $1.2 billion in assets. With crude pressing toward the upper end of its 52-week range between $60.67 and $98.83 per share, daily trading volumes climbed as retail investors rushed in alongside institutional players. At around $96 per share, USO sat near levels that would test its year-long ceiling.

### What traders are debating now

The debate running through trading desks is whether this week's move reflects a structural shift in the oil market or simply a short-term fear premium embedded into crude prices. Some analysts believe the combination of Hormuz disruptions and halted LNG output could sustain elevated prices through the rest of the year. Others argue that diplomatic de-escalation or new production entering the market could cap the rally before it extends much further.

Two variables dominate analyst projections going forward. Whether WTI holds above $70 per barrel and whether the Hormuz disruption lasts are the figures that will determine if USO's current run holds any staying power. Neither is easy to forecast when the inputs are geopolitical rather than economic.

What is clear is that oil has not lost its ability to move markets without warning. Energy continues to underpin a vast portion of the global economy in ways that technology headlines tend to obscure. Freight still moves on diesel. Factories still run on hydrocarbons. Airlines still burn jet fuel by the millions of gallons every day.

USO is not a long-term wealth builder by design, and its five-year track record confirms that plainly. But as a window into the immediate pulse of global energy markets, few instruments close the gap between world events and investor portfolios quite as fast.
